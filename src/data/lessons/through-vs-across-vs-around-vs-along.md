# Through vs Across vs Around vs Along

These four prepositions are all about **movement and space**, but they describe completely different directions and environments. The easiest way to master them is to visualize *how* you are moving through a space.

Here is the breakdown of how and when to use each one.

## Quick Comparison Table

| Preposition | Visual Concept | Best Used For... |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Through** | Inside a 3D tunnel/cube | Tunnels, forests, crowds, tall grass, tubes |
| **Across** | Over a flat 2D surface | Roads, rivers, bridges, fields, pages |
| **Around** | Circular or surrounding | Centers, obstacles, general neighborhood exploration |
| **Along** | Parallel to a line | Rivers, roads, shorelines, hallways |

> **Pro Tip:** If you can picture yourself surrounded by the object on all sides (like water, trees, or a building), use **through**. If you picture yourself standing on top of it looking at the other side (like a soccer field or a street), use **across**.

---

## 1. Through

**When to use it:** Use *through* when you enter one side of a **three-dimensional object, a container, or an enclosed space** and exit out the other side. Think of it like moving *inside* something surrounded by walls, trees, or boundaries.

* **Example 1:** The train went **through** the dark tunnel.
* **Example 2:** We had to hike **through** the thick forest to get to the cabin.

## 2. Across

**When to use it:** Use *across* when you are moving from one side of a **flat, two-dimensional surface or a clear line** to the opposite side. Think of it like drawing a line from point A to point B over an open area.

* **Example 1:** She swam **across** the wide river.
* **Example 2:** The cat ran right **across** the busy street.

## 3. Around

**When to use it:** Use *around* when you are moving in a **circle or a curved path surrounding a central point**, or when you are avoiding an obstacle by going past its edges. It can also mean moving aimlessly to different parts of a general area.

* **Example 1:** We walked **around** the park looking for the lost keys. *(Moving to various parts within the area)*
* **Example 2:** Drive **around** the traffic circle and take the third exit. *(Moving in a circular path)*

## 4. Along

**When to use it:** Use *along* when you are moving in a **line that follows the edge, side, or path** of something long and thin (like a road, a river, a wall, or a beach).

* **Example 1:** We love to stroll **along** the beach at sunset.
* **Example 2:** There are beautiful old trees planted **along** the highway.
